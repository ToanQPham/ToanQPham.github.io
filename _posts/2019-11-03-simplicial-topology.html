— 1. Simplicial homology —

Simplicial homology means the study of homology of simplicial complexes. First, we define simplicial complexes.

Definition 1. An (abstract) simplicial complex consists of a set ${V}$, i.e. set of vertices, together with a set ${S}$ of subsets of ${V}$ such that
  1. ${\{v\}}$ in ${S}$ for every ${v\in V}$.
  2. If ${\sigma \in S}$ and ${\emptyset \ne \tau \subset \sigma \in S}$ then ${\tau \in S}$, i.e. ${S}$ is closed under operation of taking nonempty subsets.
Elements of ${S}$ are called simplices. If ${\sigma=\{v_0,\ldots, v_n\}\in S}$ then ${\sigma}$ is ${n}$-simplex and ${n\ge 0}$ is the dimension of ${\sigma}$. The dimension of the simplicial complex is the largest dimension of a simplex in ${K}$. Vertices (i.e. elements of ${V}$) are identified with ${0}$-simplices. Denote ${K^r}$ as set of all ${r}$-simplices in ${K}$.

For a ${n}$-simplex ${\sigma}$, any ${\emptyset \ne \tau \subset \sigma}$ of dimension ${r}$ is called a ${r}$-face of ${\sigma}$. The union of all ${(n-1)}$-faces is called the boundary of the simplex.

Example 2. Some examples of ${k}$-simplex, ${k}$-simplicial complex.

The goal of homology is to count number of "holes" of some geometric objects, such as torus, Klein bottle, or graph, ... In simplicial context, a "hole" is a combination of simplices which could possibly be the boundary of a simplex or of a combination of simplices, but is not. For example, the ${2}$-simplex has a boundary (according to definition) which is the set of its three edges. However, the set of three edges of a simplicial circle is not a boundary, and geometrically, it forms a "hole".

Another example is the simplicial torus which has three uppermost edges looking just like the boundary of a ${2}$-simplex but there is no ${2}$-simplex in this simplicial torus. That's how we realize there's a hole in the torus.

To define homology groups, we need an orientation for our complex.

Definition 3 (Orientation). An orientation of a simplex is an ordering of vertices of that simplex. An orientation of a simplicial complex is a choice of orientation of each simplex. If ${\{v_0,\ldots,v_k\}}$ spans ${k}$-simplex ${\sigma}$ then we will use the symbol ${[v_0,\ldots, v_k]}$ to denote the oriented simplex.

There are many ways to define an orientation of a simplicial complex ${K=(V,S)}$. For example, one can define a total ordering on the vertex set ${V}$ and induce an ordering for each simplex whose vertex set is a subset of ${V}$. In particular, if our ordered vertex set is ${V=\{v_0,\ldots, v_n\}}$ then ${[v_{a_0},\ldots,v_{a_k}]}$ is an oriented simplex iff ${a_i<a_j}$ for any ${i<j}$. Simplcial complex with this orientation is called ordered simplicial complex. This will be our choice for the definition of orientation.

One can also define a partial ordering on ${V}$ that induces a total ordering on each simplex of ${K}$.

Another way is that we define the orientation of an ${n}$-simplex ${[v_0,v_1,\ldots, v_n]}$ to be an equivalence class of orderings of the vertices where two orderings are equivalence if and only if one can be obtained from the other by an even permutation. For example, ${[v_0,v_1,v_2,v_3]=[v_1,v_0,v_3,v_2]= -[v_0,v_2,v_1,v_3]}$. \footnote{Most books/notes use the last definition for orientation but by doing this, one would have to prove well-defineness (since we are dealing will equivalence classes) for any other definitions using the orientation.}

The only purpose in defining the orientation of simplices is to define the boundary of a chain (or more specifically, to define the face maps).

Definition 5 (${r}$-chain group). For a simplcial complex ${K}$ with dimension ${n}$, for ${0 \le r \le n}$ then the ${r}$-chain group ${C_r(K)}$ is the set consisting of elements, called ${r}$-chain of the form ${\sum_{\sigma\in K^r}\lambda \sigma}$ where ${\lambda\in {\mathbb Z}}$ and the sum is over all ${r}$-simplices ${\sigma}$. In particular, ${C_r(K)}$ is a free abelian group generated by ${r}$-simplices.

Definition 6 (Boundary maps). We define a boundary homomorphism ${\partial_r:C_r(K) \rightarrow C_{r-1}(K)}$ on the generators of ${C_r(K)}$ by

$\displaystyle \partial_r[v_0,v_1,\ldots,v_r]= \sum_{i=0}^{r}(-1)^i [v_0,\ldots,\widehat{v_i},\ldots,v_r]$

where the oriented simplex ${[v_0,\ldots,\widehat{v_i},\ldots,v_r]}$ is the ${i}$-th face of ${[v_0,\ldots,v_r]}$, obtained by deleting ${i}$-th vertex.

By convention, ${\partial_0([v_i])=0}$ for all possible ${i}$.

Remark 7. If with definition 3 of orientation as equivalence classes obtained by taking even permutation, then ${\partial_r}$ is well-defined.

Proposition 8. For simplicial complex ${K}$ then ${\partial_r \circ \partial_{r+1}=0}$.

The boundary of an ${(r+1)}$-simplex, which is a set of ${r+1}$ ${r}$-simplices, which can be represented as an ${r}$-chain through the boundary homomorphism, is in the image of the boundary homomorphism ${\partial_{r+1}}$. This suggests the name for elements of ${\text{im}\partial_{r+1}}$ as ${r}$-boundaries. As ${\text{im} \partial_{r+1} \subset \ker \partial_r}$, ${\ker \partial_r}$ is the set of ${r}$-chains that could potentially be boundary and we call elements of ${\ker \partial_r}$ as ${r}$-cycles. Recall that in homology, we would like to know which combination of simplices that could be a boundary but is not, i.e. ${r}$-chains in ${\ker \partial_{n}}$ that is not in ${\partial_{n+1}}$. Hence, one way to do this is take the quotient group ${\ker \partial_r/\text{im} \partial_{r+1}}$, which will be the definition of homology group:

Definition 9. The quotient ${H_r(K)=\ker \partial_r/\text{im} \partial_{r+1}}$ is called the ${r}$-th homology group. Two ${r}$-cycles ${\alpha, \beta}$ are homologous if the ${r}$-cycle ${\alpha-\beta}$ is a ${r}$-boundary. The equivalence class of a ${r}$-cycle ${\alpha}$ is the homology class ${[\alpha]}$.

Example 10. Consider simplicial complex ${K}$ of ${3}$ vertices ${v_0,v_1,v_2}$ and set of simplices ${\{[v_0,v_1], [v_1,v_2],[v_0,v_2],[v_0],[v_1],[v_2]\}}$.

— 1.1. Q: Does simplicial homology depends on orientation? —

If our chosen definition of orientation based on a total ordering of the vertices then the answer is no. \href{MSE}} Let ${K}$ be a simplicial complex and denote ${K_1}$ and ${K_2}$ the complexes obtained from ${K}$ with two different orientations then the simplicial homology groups of ${K_1}$ and ${K_2}$ are isomorphic. That is, for each ${n}$, we have ${H_n(K_1) \cong H_n(K_2)}$.
